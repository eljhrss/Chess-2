BOARD ANATOMY

A Chessboard consists of approximately 64 squares, made up with an 8X8 Grid.

It alternates colors for distinction. Files are vertical columns numbered 1-8, while

Ranks are horizontal rows labeled from A-H. In the given picture, we can

determine a piece's position by combining the letter and the number,

giving us positions like A8, H1, B2, etc.

It is important to note that the white pieces are placed in 1st and 2nd Ranks,

while the black pieces are placed in 7th and 8th Ranks. The term king's side

refers to E-H files, while queen's side refers to A-D files. If there are no labels

and numbers on the chessboard, remember to have a light square at the

right-hand corner.

MOVING PIECES

The King is the most important piece, determining your chance of winning

or losing. It has a cross on its top and moves one square in any direction,

whether vertical, horizontal, or diagonal. It is a highly valued piece typically

inactive in the game until much later to protect it from Checkmate,

which ends the game.

The Queen is considered as the most powerful piece. It appears as a

crown-like piece that has the capability to move multiple squares in any

direction, whether vertical, horizontal, or diagonal. It is valued

highly with 9 points, making it a crucial part of the game.

The Rook appears to have a fortress-like top and moves in multiple squares

vertically or horizontally It is valued with 5 points and typically becomes active

during endgame. It can also protect the king by Castling, moving the king two

squares away, then putting the rook beside the king.

The Bishop appears to have a cross in its cap, and moves in multiple

squares diagonally. It is valued with 3 points and typically becomes

active early in the game.

The knight appears as a horse and moves in an L-like position.

Two squares straight, then turning to one square. It is valued

with 3 points, and the only piece in the game that can jump

over pieces, which makes it a piece you can move at the

very start of the game.

Pawns fill the second row, covering the other pieces and can be moved one

square, or two squares forward, but in the corresponding moves,

only one square forward. It captures diagonally. It is the least

valued with 1 point, but when promoted (moving pawn to

other side of the chessboard), it becomes important.

HOW TO WIN/ADDITIONAL RULES

To win the game, a player must Checkmate the opponent, and is referred as the

King in Check (having a piece pointed directly at the king, with the king needed

to be protected), with no possible moves. To get out of a Check, player needs to

either move the king, block the check, or to capture the piece that made the

check possible.

En Passant is a special pawn move where if the pawn moves two square and it

lands beside the opponent's pawn, the opponent can capture the pawn

diagonally, in this case, the black pawn captures the white pawn. This

move must be made immediately after the two-square pawn move.

The 50 move rule is a chess rule which concludes in a draw between players

making 50 consecutive moves without any pawn move or a capture.

The 3-fold repetition rule is a chess rule which also concludes in a draw

if players make the same moves repeatedly at least 3 times.

Insufficient Material in chess also concludes in a draw because of the

inability to checkmate the opponent's king with the remaining pieces.

Stalemate happens when player has no legal moves,

while their king is not in check, resulting in a draw.

The Touch-move rule is characterized by players not being able to touch their

pieces and move it somewhere, but returning it to the old position. They must

move it where they want it to land, and touching an opponent's piece would

mean the player must capture that exact piece.
